EEG17: Normal variants

Probably the most difficult part of reading EEGs is recognizing normal features. This often translates into unexperienced EEG readers calling some findings in the EEG as abnormal when they are actually normal. Unexperienced EEG readers tend to "overcall" (call anything that looks funny as abnormal) and this may lead to bad consequences for their patients. More experienced readers are able to recognize "abnormal looking" features in the EEG which are still normal. An important part of becoming an experienced EEG reader is recognizing normal variants: features in the EEG which look "abnormal", "funny", "unusual", but they are normal. Let's go for (some of) them!



Adults get away with this! (normal variants in adults)

There are many funny-looking features in the EEG of adults, which are completely normal.



One of the most striking features of reading adult EEGs (at least for a pediatric neurologist) is the very low-amplitude, very poorly sustained posterior dominant rhythm in a large proportion of adults.









It can be because of thickening of the skull bone with age. The older the patient, the more likely it is to see this type of posterior dominant rhythm. It looks like the posterior dominant rhythm fades with age and ends up being a shadow of what it was during the younger years. That also happens to some people when they get older. In any case, it is completely normal, a normal variant.

Babies get away with this! (normal variants in newborns)

Babies get away with a lot. Their EEG is fundamentally different than the EEG at later ages.

The EEG in newborns does not have most of the normal features of EEG in older ages (posterior dominant rhythm, vertex waves, sleep spindles), some features are normal but would be very abnormal in EEGs from older people (discontinuity, sharp transients), and the normal variants vary widely within few weeks of gestational age. Neonatal EEG is a completely different (and complex) topic which deserves its own space.









SUMMARY

Normal variants: weird-looking, but normal.

The normal variants above are the most frequent ones and you might want to learn them to not overcall them as abnormal.

Apart from that, there are general rules that will help you identify them: 1) if they have always the same appearance (monomorphic) they are probably a normal variant; 2) if they are not potentiated by stage II sleep, they are probably a normal variant; 3) if they do not break the rhythm, they are probably a normal variant; and 4) if they appear in runs or trains with a frequency of 6 Hz or higher, they are probably a normal variant. Extra tip: if they appear isolated, but their morphology is the same than when they appear in runs or trains in the same EEG, then the isolated element is probably a normal variant.

Let's keep learning!
