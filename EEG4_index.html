EEG4: Epileptiform activity

Doctors who are not familiar with EEG think that EEG is used to "look for epileptiform activity". Obviously, that is an oversimplification because there is much more information in an EEG than just whether there is epileptiform activity or not. However, it is true that one of the major uses of EEG is to look for epileptiform activity.

Epileptiform activity (or lack thereof) is one of the major highlights of an EEG. The presence or absence of epileptiform activity frequently modifies medical management. When present, epileptiform activity may hint at the mechanism of onset of seizures. As a crucial part of EEG which critically influences clinical management, you would expect that epileptiform activity has an objective and clear definition that allows all readers to agree on recognizing it and differentiate epileptiform activity from other EEG elements. Are you ready for the definition? Well, ... there is no universal definition of epileptiform activity. Sorry!

Yes, identifying epileptiform activity is an imperfect process mainly driven by pattern recognition based on prior experience, with no clear-cut definition of what is and what is not epileptiform activity. Epileptiform activity is typically defined as an EEG element that is "pointy" or "sharp" and "breaks the underlying EEG rhythm". If there was a prize for the most unclear, subjective, and unsatisfactory definition ever, I would definitively vote for the definition of epileptiform activity. If one tries to go deeper into the definition and asks what does it mean "pointy" or "sharp"? or how "pointy" or "sharp" it has to be to be epileptiform activity? the best answer you will get is something like "it is sharp enough that if you sit on it, it will hurt". If you ask what does "break the rhythm" mean? the best answer you will get is something like "it is markedly different from the prior and subsequent EEG". To make things better, not all EEG elements that are "sharp" and "break the rhythm" are epileptiform activity. I warned you that trying to learn to identify epileptiform activity based on definitions will lead you nowhere.

The good news is that using a few simple rules of thumb that you will learn in this post, you can easily identify epileptiform activity. Let's learn how to recognize epileptiform activity!





Some features are typical or characteristic of epileptiform activity. These include:





EPILEPTIFORM ACTIVITY BREAKS THE RHYTHM


As other defining features of epileptiform activity, "breaks the rhythm" is quite subjective. It means that epileptiform activity "stands out", "does not fit" with the rest of the rhythms in the EEG.











Explaining "breaks the rhythm" is difficult in words, but may be more intuitive in pictures.























EPILEPTIFORM ACTIVITY IS SHARPLY CONTOURED


Epileptiform activity is sharply contoured. Yes, that is very unspecific, but it is one of the first features you should look for. If an EEG element is not sharply-contoured it is unlikely to be epileptiform activity.






There is a wide variability in the sharpness of epileptiform activity.





Something that can make "sharply contoured" more specific for epileptiform activity (rather than other EEG elements which are also sharply contoured but are not epileptiform activity) is the pattern of steepness. You can think of the sharply-contoured part of epileptiform activity as consisting of 2 lines that join in the pointy part. In most cases, in epileptiform activity, the first part is steeper than the second part.







This difference in steepness is subtle and sometimes more difficult to find than just finding a sharply contoured element. However, it is also more specific too. First try to find sharply contoured elements in the EEG. Then, if you find the first line steeper than the second that makes it more likely that these elements are epileptiform activity. If you do not find this pattern of steepness, it still can be epileptiform activity. These are just rules of thumb, not definitive criteria and you should keep looking for other features before making a final decision on whether what you see is or is not epileptiform activity.







EPILEPTIFORM ACTIVITY HAS A FIELD (WHICH MAKES ANATOMICAL SENSE)


You can think of epileptiform activity as a stone falling on water creating ripples around it. The ripples are big around the point of entry of the stone in the water, but they get smaller as they get farther away.


By the way, this has not much to do with the underlying physiology, but it is an intuitive way to understand it.



Now that we are at it, you can think of little arrows pointing to the center of the ripples (where the stone entered the water).


I think I already mentioned that this does not represent the underlying physiology.



How does this apply to the fields of epileptiform activity in the EEG?













If you pay attention to the montage we are using, we are comparing electrodes in an anterior-posterior direction: we are comparing C4 to P4, then P4 to O2; similarly, we are comparing F8 to T8, then T8 to P8. This is an anterior-posterior bipolar montage, likely the most commonly used montage in EEG reading. In the example above, it provided good localization of the stone in an anterior-posterior direction. However, as you could see in the prior figures, it did not give much information on the location of the stone in a medial-lateral direction. Let's try with another example.



















If you think of the stone as a big negative charge in the scalp and the ripples as negative charges of smaller amplitudes as they get farther from the stone, that resembles as little bit more the underlying physiology.





As an aside, the epileptiform activity as small arrows pointing to the stone (the biggest negative charge) only works for bipolar montages (montages which compare one electrode to the neighbor electrode). In case of a referential montage (a montage which compares each electrode with a "reference"), it is even easier: you can think of epileptiform activity just bigger near the stone (the biggest negative charge) and smaller as it gets farther.







Localization in EEG is easier than it may seem in this rough and fast introduction to localization. However, the focus of this post is on features that help identify epileptiform activity. Most epileptiform activity has a field that makes some anatomical sense, that is, the negative charge in the scalp has a maximum somewhere and gradually becomes less negative in adjacent electrodes. The field does not have to be perfectly symmetric, but it has to make some physiological sense. If the field has maximum negativity in one electrode and no other negativity in adjacent electrodes, in most cases, it is not going to be epileptiform activity.



Q1. Identify the correct answer below:


All epileptiform activity breaks the rhythm and is sharply contoured and has a field that makes anatomical sense.
All sharply contoured elements in the EEG are epileptiform activity.
Breaking the rhythm, being sharply contoured, and having a field that makes anatomical sense are only rules of thumb that help to identify epileptiform activity.









SUMMARY

Remember these very simple rules of thumb:
Does it break the rhythm?
Is it sharply contoured?
Does it have a field that makes some anatomical sense?
The more you answer "yes" to these questions, the more likely that you are looking at epileptiform activity. Sounds simple and it is, but this will help enormously when you are trying to identify epileptiform discharges.
