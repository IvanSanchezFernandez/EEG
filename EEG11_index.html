EEG11: Seizures!

Seizures are the most characteristic feature of epilepsy.

In many patients seizures are (fortunately) rare and, therefore, rarely captured on EEG. However, when seizures are captured on EEG, they provide unparalleled information on the seizure type, seizure duration, lateralization and potential localization of the seizure onset focus, and (if the patient appears in the video during the seizure) the correlation between EEG findings and clinical semiology. Some seizures or combination of seizures help define the epilepsy syndrome of the patient.

In a perfect world, all seizures would have the same EEG defining characteristics and would be straightforward to identify when they occur. Needless to say that in the real world, there are no universal defining characteristics of a seizure and there are many different EEG patterns which can be found during a seizure.

Let's try to learn the most typical ones.

Typical absence seizures are characterized by generalized onset 3 Hz spike-wave complexes. The onset and end of these seizures is typically abrupt with a sudden transition between the normal EEG background and the seizure. Because of that, typical absence seizures are relatively easy to identify. They are so typical and characteristic that they are shown as the first example of a seizure in textbooks in medical school.

Even this very typical pattern has a couple of characteristics which is a little bit irregular. Sometimes, the seizure starts with a lead-in of 1-2 seconds wich can be more focal or lateralized. Also, the frequency is not always 3 Hz, but can evolve from approximately 4 Hz at the beginning of the seizure to approximately 2 Hz towards the end of the seizure.

SUMMARY

After reviewing this post, you have learned a little bit about the most frequent EEG patterns found in a seizure. There are multiple variations of the seizure patterns above (for example, seizures with fast polyspikes can be focal, focal seizures can generalize, etc.). Also, there are many other EEG patterns found in seizures. However, learning the patterns above is a good starting point.

Hopefully, you learned that a seizure typically evolves in frequency (rhythmicity changes its frequency over time), amplitude (the EEG pattern changes in amplitude over time), and the area affected by the seizure tends to change (typically spread) over time.

Evolution is probably the most defining characteristic of a seizure and the only feature that is common to all seizure types.
