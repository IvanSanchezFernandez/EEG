Montages

To understand localization you first need a good understanding of EEG montages.







The EEG measures voltage. In particular, the EEG measures the difference in voltage between two electrodes.















Wait...Why do negative differences go up? It is just that the first companies creating electrophysiological machines decided that negative differences would be represented as upgoing deflections (and positive differences as downgoing deflections). There is no particular reason for it and it is not particularly intuitive, but it is too late to change it (and also, making it the other way around would also be as arbitrary as it is now). The first electrophysiological machine makers arbitrarily decided this convention for us.







Bipolar montages do not have to be in an anterior-posterior direction. For example, transverse montages compare one electrode to the one lateral to it.





Different montages complement each other because the electrodes which are end-of-chain in one bipolar montage are typically not end-of-chain in another bipolar montage. For example, transverse bipolar montages are particularly helpful to look for phase reversals in electrodes which are end-of-chain in anterior-posterior bipolar montages. Transverse bipolar montages are also typically used during sleep, because they nicely show the symmetry of vertex waves, sleep spindles, and other sleep elements.







Another bipolar montage is the circumferential montage, in which electrodes are compared to the next electrode forming a circle. The circumferential bipolar montage, as other bipolar montages allow a different perspective when comparing electrodes.







And what is that referential thing? A referential montage is based on the idea of using some electrode (or group of electrodes) that is (are) "electrically inactive": there should not be major deflections in the referential electrode(s). A common reference is Cz because it is in the midline far from head muscles and where epileptiform discharges or seizures are relatively rare.

Q3. Why does the EEG display electrical activity generated in the brain at all? Electrical activity of much higher voltage (for example, electrical activity generated in the heart or electrical activity generated by machines around the patient) should contaminate and obscure the tiny electrical activity coming from the brain, right?


The electrical activity coming from the brain is actually of higher voltage than the electrical activity coming from the heart or from machines around the patient
The electrical currents generated in the brain, although tiny, generate a difference in voltage between different electrodes in the scalp. In contrast, electrical currents generated in the heart or in machines around the patient, although of much higher voltage, arrive at the electrodes on the scalp with similar voltage, and the difference in voltage between the electrodes is zero (cancels out)
The electrical activity generated in the brain is of completely different frequencies than the electrical activity coming from the heart, from machines around the patient, or from any other noise

Localization

Now we know what we are measuring on EEG: differences in voltage between scalp electrodes, how we are measuring it: using different EEG montages, and what it means: an underlying electrical dipole that, in many cases, is perpendicular to the surface of the scalp and roughly located below the point of maximum scalp negativity. Equipped with all that knowledge, we can now understand how to localize deflections on the EEG.























What about seizures? You should localize seizures roughly the same way. Look at the deflections and estimate where the maximum negativity on scalp EEG is. Seizures are a succession of evolving deflections, so the localization varies with time. The localization of greatest interest in most seizures is the localization of the seizure onset.
