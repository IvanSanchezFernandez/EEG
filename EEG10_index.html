EEG10: Slowing

Slowing occurs when the EEG rhythms are slower than expected.

What is slower than expected? When is there too much slowing? It is complicated!

In this post we will learn when slowing is abnormal and how to classify slowing.



FOCAL SLOWING








Slowing that occurs locally in some regions of the head is termed focal slowing. Focal slowing is relatively easy to identify because the normal EEG should look symmetric across the midline. Therefore, asymmetries catch the reader's eye and help identify focal slowing.

Key concept: Focal slowing marks an area of structurally or functionally abnormal brain. Areas of focal slowing often have admixed epileptiform activity within the slowing. It is also frequent to see areas of focal slowing without admixed epileptiform discharges and, at some point earlier or later in the EEG, appearance of epileptiform discharges in the same area of the brain. Focal slowing and focal epileptiform activity are different and mean different things (focal epileptiform discharges: a decreased threshold for focal seizures in that area; focal slowing: focal area of the brain which is functionally or structurally abnormal). Focal epileptiform discharges and focal slowing can occur independently: an abnormal area of the brain is not necessarily epileptogenic, but focal epileptiform discharges and focal slowing occur in the same area more often than expected by chance.









Focal slowing can be intermittent focal slowing, when the slowing affects an area of the brain sometimes, but other times the same area of the brain shows normal rhythms or continuous focal slowing, when the focal slowing occurs all the time or most of the time. This distinction is not particularly specific for the etiology. Postictal focal slowing tends to appear only once after a seizure and then disappear, but other etiologies like tumors or strokes can appear as intermittent focal slowing or continuous focal slowing.

GENERALIZED SLOWING


Generalized slowing occurs when the EEG rhythms are slower than expected for age, stage in the wake-sleep cycle, and physiological condition and this abnormal slowing affects all areas of the brain diffusely.

As with focal slowing, generalized slowing can be intermittent generalized slowing, when the generalized slowing appears sometimes, but other times the brain shows normal rhythms or continuous generalized slowing, when the generalized slowing occurs all the time or most of the time. This distinction is not particularly specific for the etiology. Both intermittent and continuous generalized slowing mean diffuse dysfunction of the brain and the etiologies causing that can be very broad.

SUMMARY

After reviewing this post, you have learned a lot about slowing. Slowing can affect the whole brain (generalized slowing) or only some local areas (focal slowing). Slowing can appear every now and then (intermittent slowing) or it can appear all the time or most of the time (continuous slowing).

That gives us the four types of slowing: Intermittent focal slowing
Continuous focal slowing
Intermittent generalized slowing
Continuous generalized slowing
These types of slowing can appear alone or in combination with epileptiform activity. Different types of slowing can also appear in the same patient (for example, a patient with generalized slowing and a focal slowing on top of it or a patient with focal slowing in several areas of the brain).

Before calling any type of slowing as abnormal, make sure to rule out that the slowing is not physiological (young age, sleep, hyperventilation...) or an artifact (patient brushing teeth, cough, machines in the intensive care unit...).
