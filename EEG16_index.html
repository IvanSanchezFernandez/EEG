EEG16: Artifacts!

You have already seen some of the most common artifacts. It is extremely important to recognize EEG artifacts to avoid confusing them with brain activity. Let's review artifacts more systematically.

First of all: what is an artifact? An artifact is an EEG pattern that is not originated from brain activity. It can be caused by other parts of the body (for example, contraction of an skeletal muscle, heart electrical activity, etc.) or by machines around the body (for example, ventilators in the ICU, a cell phone around the patient, etc.).

Most artifacts can be recognized as not originating from the brain because of their non-physiological looking pattern (they do not look like the rest of brain activity), but their greatest danger is when they are not very different than epileptiform activity or even seizures, which leads to misclassification of a normal EEG as pathologic.

SUMMARY

You have seen the most common EEG artifacts.

You have probably realized that artifacts share the common feature that they "do not look physiologic", they look different than activity coming from the brain. The most obvious artifacts can be quickly recognized by their pattern and their "non-physiologic" appearance. When in doubt, localize the origin of the elements you see on EEG. If the localization does not make much sense, then it is probably an artifact. That is one of the reasons this post about artifacts comes just after the post on localization.

Let's keep learning things that will make us better EEG readers.
