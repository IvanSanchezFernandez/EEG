INTERICTAL EPILEPTIFORM ACTIVITY


Interictal epileptiform activity occurs in (a small proportion of) persons who never had seizures and will never have seizures. Ordering routine EEGs on patients with events which are not seizures will not clarify much. Interictal epileptiform activity in this context will probably represent an incidental finding which should not be treated. Remember: tests cannot replace a proper clinical history.

The first step in diagnosing seizures and epilepsy is taking a proper clinical history, the second step in diagnosing seizures and epilepsy is taking a proper clinical history, the third step in diagnosing seizures and epilepsy is taking a proper clinical history. Only after making sure that you took a proper clinical history and made a proper physical exam, ordering an EEG (or not ordering an EEG) should include answers to the questions: how the EEG will contribute to the diagnosis? is the proper EEG a routine EEG (likely to capture interictal abnormalities, but unlikely to capture ictal events) or a prolonged EEG (more likely to catpure ictal events)? if the EEG shows interictal discharges, how would that change (or not) the diagnosis and treatment?

SUMMARY

EEG is a very helpful test. However, EEG is also a test with many limitations. EEGs do not "rule out seizures". EEGs do not "rule out epilepsy". Interictal discharges on EEG can happen in persons who never had a seizure and will never have a seizure. Therefore, it is surprising that EEGs are used in certain environments to "rule out seizures", to "rule out epilepsy", or to make a diagnosis of epilepsy when interictal epileptiform are found (regardless of clinical context). The popularity of the term "seizure disorder" is particularly worrying because it directly leads to patient harm (antiseizure medications in patients who do not need them or incorrect treatment duration in patients who do need them).

Your colleague in the questions above represented some of the most common misconceptions in the use of EEG. Unfortunately, these misconceptions harm patients because: 1) there are patients who have epilepsy, but their epilepsy is "ruled out" by a normal EEG and, therefore, these patients do not receive the antiseizure medications they need, sometimes for years and 2) there are patients who have not epilepsy, but the finding of interictal epileptiform activity makes them receive antiseizure medications they do not need, sometimes for years.

Make sure you understand these simple but powerful concepts and do not fall into the common mistakes your colleague fell into.
