EEG2: The posterior dominant rhythm

The posterior dominant rhythm is one of the defining features of wakefulness. The posterior dominant rhythm is the idle rhythm of the visual cortex. That is, it appears when the visual cortex is idle (the patient is calm and with eyes closed or not fixating in any object). Because the posterior dominant rhytm is generated in the visual cortex, it is better seen in the posterior channels of the EEG (those that include occipital electrodes).



THE "ALPHA RHYTHM"


Many EEG readers (especially adult neurologists and adult epileptologists) refer to the posterior dominant rhythm as "alpha rhythm". Why is that? Let's introduce the main frequencies that can be seen on EEG.


Beta frequencies >13 Hz
Alpha frequencies 8-13 Hz
Theta frequencies 4-7 Hz
Delta frequencies <3 Hz




As you can see in the table above, an alpha rhythm is one in which the wave appears 8-13 times per second (8-13 Hz). In adults, the normal posterior dominant rhythm has a frequency of 8-13 Hz, so that is the reason many adult neurologists refer to the posterior dominant rhythm as "alpha rhythm".






So far, it appears that "alpha rhythm" would be an appropriate term to refer to the posterior dominant rhythm.

WHY THE POSTERIOR DOMINANT RHYTHM SHOULD NOT BE NAMED "ALPHA RHYTHM"


The posterior dominant rhythm should not be termed "alpha rhythm" for two main reasons:
1. Even in adults (where the normal posterior dominant rhythm is in the alpha frequency), a slow (abnormal) posterior dominant rhythm may be slower than 8 Hz and, therefore, in the theta or even delta frequencies.
2. The normal posterior dominant rhythm frequency increases with age in children until it reaches the adult normal frequencies.





THE EVOLUTION OF THE POSTERIOR DOMINANT RHYTHM AT DIFFERENT AGES


The posterior dominant rhythm evolves during the first years of life until reaching its adult form.

AMPLITUDE


The normal posterior dominant rhythm has a voltage between 20 microvolts and 120 microvolts. Younger children have high-voltage posterior dominant rhythm (around 80-120 microvolts) and this voltage gets progressively smaller with age until reaching values around 20-60 microvolts in young adults. As people age, their posterior dominant rhythm becomes of lower amplitude.













SYMMETRY


The posterior dominant rhythm should be symmetric. It is normal and acceptable that sometimes the posterior dominant rhythm would appear better formed on one hemisphere than in the other hemisphere, but these differences should even out in the long run.
If the posterior dominant rhythm is persistently asymmetric, it should be considered an abnormality.
