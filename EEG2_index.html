EEG2: The posterior dominant rhythm

The posterior dominant rhythm is one of the main defining features of wakefulness. The posterior dominant rhythm is the idle rhythm of the visual cortex. That is, the posterior dominant rhythm appears when the visual cortex is idle (the patient is calm and with eyes closed or not fixating in any object).
This post will discuss the main characteristics of the posterior dominant rhythm.



THE "ALPHA RHYTHM"


Many EEG readers (especially adult neurologists and adult epileptologists) refer to the posterior dominant rhythm as "alpha rhythm". Why is that? Let's introduce the names of the main frequencies that can be seen on EEG.


Gamma frequencies >13 Hz
Alpha frequencies 8-13 Hz
Theta frequencies 4-7 Hz
Delta frequencies 1-3 Hz




As you can see in the table above, an alpha rhythm is a rhythm that occurs 8-13 times per second (8-13 Hz). In adults, the normal posterior dominant rhythm has a frequency of 8-13 Hz, so that is the reason many adult neurologists refer to the posterior dominant rhythm as "alpha rhythm".






So far, it appears that "alpha rhythm" would be an appropriate term to refer to the posterior dominant rhythm.

WHY THE POSTERIOR DOMINANT RHYTHM SHOULD NOT BE NAMED "ALPHA RHYTHM"


The posterior dominant rhythm should not be termed "alpha rhythm" for two main reasons:
1. Even in adults (where the normal posterior dominant rhythm is in the alpha frequency), a slow (abnormal) posterior dominant rhythm may be slower than 8 Hz and, therefore, in the theta or even delta frequencies.
2. The normal posterior dominant rhythm frequency increases with age in children until it reaches the adult normal frequencies.





THE EVOLUTION OF THE POSTERIOR DOMINANT RHYTHM OVER DIFFERENT AGES


The posterior dominant rhythm evolves during the first years of life until reaching its adult form.

AMPLITUDE


The posterior dominant rhythm has a voltage between approximately 20 microvolts and 120 microvolts. Younger children have high-voltage posterior dominant rhythm (around 80-120 microvolts) and this voltage gets progressively smaller with age until reaching values around 20-40 microvolts in young adults.





SYMMETRY


The posterior dominant rhythm should be symmetric. It is normal and acceptable that sometimes it appears better formed on one hemisphere than in the other hemisphere, but these differences should even out in the long run.
If the posterior dominant rhythm is persistently asymmetric, it should be considered an abnormality.

SUMMARY

After reviewing this post, you are able to identify the most important characteristics of the posterior dominant rhythm.
The posterior dominant rhythm is one of the main rhythms of wakefulness and occurs when the patient is calm with eyes closed. The frequency of the posterior dominant rhythm varies with age.




6 months of age >Abnormal if <4 Hz
12 months of age Abnormal if <5 Hz
6 years of age Abnormal if <6 Hz
8 years of age or older Abnormal if <8 Hz


The posterior dominant rhythm has to be approximately symmetric between the right and left hemispheres. If there is a persistent asymmetry, this can represent a structural lesion or a functional anomaly.
